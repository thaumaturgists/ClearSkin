The Riemann Hypothesis is a famous unsolved problem in mathematics that deals with the distribution of prime numbers. In simple terms, it states that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function (a complex function that encodes information about prime numbers) lie on a specific line in the complex plane, known as the "critical line."

To prove the Riemann Hypothesis, mathematicians would need to:

Understand the Riemann Zeta Function: Gain a deeper understanding of the properties and behavior of the zeta function, especially its zeros.
Find a Proof: Develop a rigorous mathematical proof that shows all non-trivial zeros of the zeta function indeed lie on the critical line (where the real part is 1/2).
Verify the Proof: Ensure that the proof is correct and can be checked by other mathematicians. This often involves peer review and scrutiny from the mathematical community.
Address Counterexamples: If any counterexamples are found, they would need to be addressed or disproven to maintain the validity of the hypothesis.
In summary, proving the Riemann Hypothesis requires a solid mathematical proof that withstands scrutiny and demonstrates the truth of the hypothesis regarding the zeros of the zeta function.
